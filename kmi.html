This is civic engagement education.

The Karsh Mentorship Initiative (KMI) believes a purposeful education is the most effective way to support the power of youth.

Nepal faces a crossroads on its pathway to universal, high-quality education. The stated aim of the budding system is to produce students capable of building the civic, material, and political infrastructure of the country. Yet a curriculum of rote learning, rather than of experiential learning, hinders this goal.

By partnering with Nepali organizations to implement and research alternative curricula, KMI contributes to a holistic education for every child.

As the Executive Director of KMI, I spent three consecutive summers living in Nepal. Alongside students, teachers, and principals, I have striven to expand three core goals:

Raising critical consciousness, cultivating leadership, and teaching teamwork.
Raising critical consciousness

“We went to a riverside locality. Although they are living ... not far from here ... their living standard is so different. Their children don't go to school, as they don't have enough money. But people around here ... they are getting every facility.”
—Pradip

Development is rarely equitable. To train the next generation of development leaders, we scaffold opportunities for students to reflect on the issues they believe are most pressing—then we encourage them to use their unique strengths to solve them. Our students have researched, written about, and taken action against issues from brain drain, to child labor, to environmental causes. We've found they leave better equipped to critique and remake their society.

"In our community... people are not being effortful— they are not joining hands together to solve the problem—because they are only doing for their benefits only. In some home, there is full of water... in my home, water facility is lacking. People... are not working together."
—Nisha

Cultivating leadership

"Leadership is not only to... say in front of all people, but to make them understand. We should not be afraid while expressing our views because all people are also equal, no? When we get the chance to sit in front and say something, then we should not be afraid."
—Nisha

We believe leadership is best developed through practice. After students identify the issue they care about, we support them in leading action to address it. They gather donations, stage rallies, talk to community members, and take direct action. Though we don't explicitly teach it, our students come to tell us that leadership is more than being in charge. It's about identifying the best path forward, and understanding how to guide a group down it.

"Leadership is not only being about a leader, but it is also about how we coordinate with the group, how we—what type of position we have with them. In leadership... we should understand our friends, our other people in front of us. We should also know how we are leading and why we are doing so for the effective cooperation."
—Sita

Teaching teamwork

"I think in books, in class, it was difficult for me to understand about team[work], but when I went to KMI camp they teach us how to be on a team and we work together... and I understand what is team[work] and what is leadership."
—Manisha

Collaboration is the skill of the 21st century. An increasingly globalized world demands working with collaborators from unfamiliar backgrounds. As students work together to address the issues they've self-determined, they develop a deeper understanding of teamwork. The result is a group of youth that is motivated to collaborate with anyone who shares their goals.

"By doing that act also I learn something... I learned that listening to others' voices... can help us to make our team stronger, and do the task perfectly and in a better way."
—Indra
